What is a Robo-Advisor?

Definition:
 A robo-advisor is a platform that uses algorithms to build personalized investment portfolios based on your financial goals and risk tolerance. For example, financial robo-advisors (FRAs) offer financial advice to users using advanced financial technology (FinTech) (D’Acunto et al., 2019).


How robo-advisory works?

Phase 1

Initiation & Profiling

Users complete online questionnaires to provide their risk tolerance, investment goals, financial situation, and time horizon. The algorithms create a risk profile.

Phase 2

Matching & Customization

Automated construction of diversified ETF portfolios tailored to the user's specific profile.

Phase 3

Monitoring & Rebalancing

The platform continuously tracks performance and automatically rebalances to maintain target risk levels.

Strategic Benefits

Behavioral Discipline

FRAs can also reduce households’ exposure to cognitive biases (D’Acunto et al., 2019, as cited in Nourallah et al., 2026). It helps reduce mistakes made by investors.

Cost Efficiency

The Financial robo-advisors (FRAs) allow households to participate in the financial market with a limited wealth without time or place constraints (Nourallah et al., 2026). It helps scaling financial advice to a mass market as households don’t have to pay expensive costs to real person advisory.

Asset Diversification

Robo-advisors benefit investors by offering diversified portfolios, helping them allocate assets for investors who lack control and access to wealth management tools (Zhang, J. 2015). It helps reduce concentration risk and helps investors reach their financial goals.

Critical Limitations

Poor Personalization

Findings show that a successful service relationship is based on answering the customers’ expectations and building trust and reliability (Eichler & Schwab, 2024). However, it can only build on continuous relationship building in a human-to-human context. Therefore, robo-advisors’ poor personalization still cannot replace a true advisor in real life.

Algorithmic Bias

Robo-advisors are limited by the quality of the data. Although a robo-advisor can reduce human intervention, their algorithms are also invented by humans. Their inventor might be influenced by their own cognitive biases. In short, a robo-advisor cannot be completely free from human effort, opinion, and cognition (Eichler & Schwab, 2024).

Emotional Oversight

Robo-advisors only provide advice with data and answers from questionnaires. However, this neglects the users' preferences and attitudes, which are also very important to investment (Eichler & Schwab, 2024).
