What is Risk Tolerance?

Risk tolerance is an investor’s ability and willingness to lose some or all of an investment in exchange for greater potential returns (U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission, n.d.).

A robo-advisor uses a client's answers to a risk questionnaire to assign a risk profile, then builds a portfolio accordingly (TD Wealth Investing, n.d.).

Factors that influence risk tolerance:

  • Investment time horizon – longer horizons can usually absorb more risk (Investopedia, 2023a).
  • Financial goals – retirement, house purchase, or emergency savings (Betterment, 2024).
  • Emotional comfort – ability to stay invested during market drops (E*TRADE from Morgan Stanley, 2024).
  • Income and net worth – higher wealth may allow more risk (Investopedia, 2023b).

Based on these factors, robo-advisors classify investors into three common profiles: Conservative, Moderate, and Aggressive (Wealthfront, 2024).

Comparing Different Strategies

Strategy Typical Asset Allocation Goal
Conservative 70–80% bonds and 20–30% stocks Keeping money safe and stable
Moderate 40–60% stocks and 40–60% bonds Safety and growth in balance
Aggressive 80–100% stocks and 0–20% bonds Maximum long-term growth
RETIREMENT & SHORT-TERM

Conservative Strategy

(Investopedia, 2023a)

Ideal for: Retired individuals or those with 1–3 year goals who do not want to take risk.

Examples: Wealthfront "Conservative Risk" / Betterment "Safety Net"

Key Features

  • Pay attention to fixed income, like government and corporate bonds (Wealthfront, 2024).
  • Minimal impact during market crashes.
  • Less expected returns, but more stable.
MOST COMMON CHOICE

Moderate Strategy

(TD Wealth Investing, n.d.)

Ideal for: Professionals in the middle of their careers who want to reach their goals in the next five to ten years.

Typical Allocation: 50% Stocks / 50% Bonds

Key Features

  • Balanced mix of stocks for growth and bonds for stability (Betterment, 2024).
  • A little bit of protection from inflation.
  • Still able to bounce back from small drops.
  • A typical robo-advisor allocation is 50% stocks from around the world and 50% bonds (Wealthfront, 2024).
LONG-TERM GROWTH

Aggressive Strategy

(Investopedia, 2023c)

Ideal for: Young investors or those with 10+ year goals who can handle more risk.

Note: High reward potential, but 30–50% loss possible in bear markets.

Key Features

  • Invest many money to domestic and international stocks (Investopedia, 2023c).
  • Includes small-caps, real estate, and emerging markets.
  • Maximum expected returns for long-term compounding.

How a Robo-Advisor Works

1

Risk Profiling

The client answers a short questionnaire. (e.g., "If your portfolio lost 15% in one month, would you sell, hold, or buy more?")

(E*TRADE from Morgan Stanley, 2024)
➔
2

Mapping to Strategy

The system assigns a score (1–10) and selects Conservative (1–3), Moderate (4–7), or Aggressive (8–10).

(TD Wealth Investing, n.d.)
➔
3

Portfolio Construction

Specific Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs) are chosen to match the target asset allocation.

(Investopedia, 2023b)
➔
4

Automatic Rebalancing

The system periodically adjusts the portfolio back to its original target to maintain risk levels.

(Betterment, 2024)
