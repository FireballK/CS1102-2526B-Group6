Quantitative Strategy Discovery

Case Study 1: Capital Preservation for HOS Premium Payment

Investor Profile: Mr. Chan, a 60-year-old nearing retirement, has just received a lump sum from his MPF. He plans to use HK$500,000 to pay for the premium of a Home Ownership Scheme (HOS) flat in 3 years.

Strategy: Conservative (80% Fixed-Income / 20% Equity).

Analytical Finding: Mr. Chan already 60 years old, and he has to use this capital to pay for the flat in 3 years, which mean he cannot accept a high risk investment. The simulation calculate HK$546,363.5. For short-term liabilities in HK's inflationary environment, low-volatility debt instruments brings lower risk, ensuring the liquidity remains intact for the HOS transaction.

Case Study 2: Real Growth for Middle-Class Housing Down Payment

Investor Profile: Ms. Wong is a 28 years old healthcare professional with HK$200,000 in initial capital, aiming to accumulate a residential down payment over a 7-year horizon.

Strategy: Moderate (50% Equity / 50% Fixed-Income).

Analytical Finding: Ms. Wong is a salaried worker who has enough risk tolerance. Her portfolio has reached HK$300,726.05. This "Balanced" strategy provides a necessary risk premium above the Composite Consumer Price Index (CCPI), which is essential in Hong Kong where housing inflation often outpaces general CPI growth.
Reference: Census and Statistics Department, HKSAR (2024). Historical analysis of Hong Kong CCPI and residential property price indices.

Case Study 3: Life-Cycle Investing for Fresh Graduates

Investor Profile: A 22-year-old CityU graduate investing a HK$20,000 into a retirement-focused portfolio with a 35-year time horizon.

Strategy: Aggressive (90% Equity / 10% Fixed-Income).

Analytical Finding: The student is a fresh university graduate. He has enough risk tolerance because he is young. This investment plan is low risk, and his invest amount is small. The terminal value exceeds HK$560,000. This proves that for young Hong Kong investors, Shortfall risk is much more harmful than market ups and downs. Therefore, young people should use a high-equity strategy early in their careers.
Reference: Bodie, Z., Merton, R. C., & Samuelson, W. F. (1992). Labor supply flexibility and portfolio choice in a life cycle model.

Technical Reflection & Future Enhancements

While this simulation provides a baseline for financial planning, future iterations will address several computational limitations:

  • Dynamic Asset Allocation: Implementing a "Glide Path" algorithm that automatically de-risks the portfolio as the target date approaches (Target-Date Fund logic).
  • Stochastic Modeling: Utilizing Monte Carlo Methods to simulate 10,000+ market paths to provide a probability distribution of outcomes rather than a deterministic linear return.
  • Friction Cost Integration: Accounting for HK-specific transaction costs (Stamp Duty, brokerage fees) and the Management Expense Ratio (MER) of local ETFs.
